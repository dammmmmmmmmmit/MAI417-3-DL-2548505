Leo woke every morning before the sun rose over the eastern mountains.
He dressed quickly and laced his warm brown leather boots with care.
Max the dog waited by the front door with his golden tail wagging fast.

The notebook had a dark brown cover worn soft and smooth from years of use.
Every single page of every notebook was filled with careful writing and drawings.
Leo had been keeping these forest notebooks for more than fifteen years.
The morning air outside was cool and full of the fresh scent of pine trees.
Max ran ahead down the narrow winding path toward the edge of the forest.
Leo followed at a slower pace and breathed the morning air very deeply.
The path was soft underfoot with a thick layer of fallen autumn leaves.

The oak was ancient and wider than four strong men standing side by side.
Leo always placed his hand gently on the rough warm bark of the oak tree.

Leo opened his notebook and began to write as he walked slowly forward.
He wrote about the light and the air and the colors and the sounds around him.
Every single morning walk gave Leo new ideas and new things to describe.
The forest path wound gently between tall trees and over smooth gray stones.

Leo loved to stop beside the stream and listen to the water moving over rocks.

Leo sat on a large flat rock beside the water and wrote more careful notes.
Morning light came through the leaves above in long and gentle golden beams.

Leo smiled and felt grateful for another peaceful morning in the green forest.

The forest had found him when he needed it most and he was grateful every day.

Wild flowers bloomed along the forest path in shades of yellow and white and purple.

Birds returned from warmer places in spring with new and joyful energy and songs.

He filled many pages in his notebook in spring with descriptions of bird calls.
Spring mornings in the forest were misty and the mist added mystery and beauty.
Beauty and mystery lived together quietly in the green and misty spring forest.
Summer arrived slowly and turned every leaf in the forest to deep dark green.
The deep green shade of summer leaves kept the forest trails pleasantly cool.
Leo walked longer distances in summer when the warm evening light lasted late.
He always stopped at the river during long summer walks to rest and cool down.
Cool river water on a hot summer day felt like one of life's finest simple gifts.
Max loved summer best because he could swim freely in the deeper river water.
The river ran slower in summer and the water was warmer and perfectly clear.

Summer evenings in the valley were long and golden and filled with pleasant birdsong.
The evening light in summer painted the entire green meadow in shades of warm gold.

The stars appeared one by one in the summer sky as the golden light faded to blue.
He packed his bag slowly at the end of each long and happy summer forest evening.
Walking home through the forest in the last summer light was always deeply peaceful.
Peace was the thing the forest gave Leo most reliably in every single season.
He was most fully himself in the summer forest with his notebook and faithful dog.
Max never grew tired of summer walks no matter how long or how far they traveled.
They walked together through meadow and forest and valley from early dawn to dusk.
The long summer days made them both strong and content and deeply satisfied.
Satisfied by simple things was how Leo described his happy summer forest life.
Simple things like water and sunlight and green leaves and birdsong and clean air.
These simple things were available to anyone willing to stop and pay attention.
Leo had learned to pay careful attention and the forest rewarded him richly for it.
Every summer felt slightly different from the summer before in small important ways.
Leo recorded these small differences faithfully in his summer notebooks each year.
Autumn painted the forest in gold and red and deep and burning orange colors.

He gathered the most beautiful fallen leaves and pressed them in his thick notebook.

He learned the tree names from old Martin who knew this forest better than anyone else.
Martin told Leo that every tree in the forest had its own story and its own spirit.
Autumn mists settled in the valley every morning and made everything soft and quiet.

But Leo always went because the winter forest had its own deep and quiet beauty.
Beauty lived in every single season of the forest and Leo understood this completely.

He had once fallen through thin ice as a younger man and he never forgot that cold day.
The cold shock of the dark water had surprised him completely and Max had barked loudly.

That cold winter day had created a deep and lasting bond between the man and his dog.
Warmth between Leo and faithful Max was the warmest thing in his entire winter life.
The mountains were white with heavy snow from October all the way through late March.
March was when the first fragile signs of spring slowly crept back into the cold valley.
Valley snowdrops and early flowers were the first welcome messengers that winter was ending.
The ending of winter was something Leo celebrated each year with a very long morning walk.
He walked the full length of the valley on the first warm morning day of every new spring.
Spring sunlight felt warmer and more welcoming after the many cold months of deep winter.
Leo celebrated the return of spring with a long and joyful entry in his brown notebook.
The notebook entry for the first spring day was always his most detailed and most hopeful.

The old oak showed small tight green buds on the first truly warm morning of early spring.

Leo photographed the oak in every season and kept all the photographs in careful albums.
His home was a small stone cottage at the southern edge of the wide and peaceful valley.
The stone cottage had a wooden porch where Leo sat every evening to watch the sunset.
He drank warm tea on the porch and wrote the final notes of each long day there quietly.
The view from the cottage porch showed the whole valley and the dark forest on the slope.
Leo had chosen this cottage specifically because of the view across the valley it offered.

It was the most beautiful view Leo had ever encountered in his long and well traveled life.
His traveling years had taken him to forests and mountains on several different continents.
But no forest he had ever walked through felt like home the way this valley forest did.
Home was the most important word in Leo's private and personal notebook vocabulary.
Words were important to Leo because words were his primary tools for recording his life.

With those simple tools he had completely filled twenty three notebooks over his years here.
The notebooks were stored very carefully in a tall dark wooden bookcase in his study.
His study had one large window looking directly at the ancient and wide old oak tree.
When he wrote at his desk he could always see the oak and the forest and the mountains.
The mountains changed their appearance depending on the light and the season and the weather.
Each season brought a completely different mountain face to observe from his study window.
Leo had painted watercolor pictures of the mountains in every season of every year.
His watercolor paintings covered most of the white walls of the small and comfortable cottage.
Visitors to the cottage always noticed the paintings first before anything else in the room.
There were very few visitors because Leo lived a quiet and largely solitary forest life.
Life in the forest valley suited his quiet and thoughtful and carefully observant nature well.
Perfectly suited to slow walking and careful watching and patient writing was Leo himself.

Evening writing sessions at his cottage desk were when his most careful observations appeared.
These sessions produced long detailed paragraphs about light and sound and smell and texture.
Time moved very differently in the forest from the way it had moved in the loud city before.
His past city life seemed impossibly fast and crowded and noisy compared to the valley pace.
Valley life had healed something in Leo that the difficult and exhausting city years had broken.
The forest asked nothing from Leo but his time and his complete and genuine daily attention.
In return the forest gave him more material for writing than he could exhaust in a full lifetime.
A lifetime in the valley could never fully capture all of its beauty and detail in words alone.
Words were limited but the forest was unlimited and this truth made Leo both glad and humble.
Happy and humble was how old Martin had once described a true forest person many years ago.
Years ago Martin had taken young Leo on his very first long and memorable walk in the valley.

Transformed by one long day in the forest was exactly how he remembered that important walk.
Martin showed him the oldest trees and the finest river pools and the deepest quiet glades.
Glades were the open and sunny spaces that appeared between the close packed forest trees.
The deep forest had its own cool and damp microclimate unlike the open and sunny valley floor.
Leo always felt a slight and pleasant temperature drop when he left the meadow for the forest.
The forest properly began where the last meadow grass ended and the first tall trees took over.
Taking over from the open meadow the old trees created a green canopy filtering all sunlight.

For Leo the library was the second most important building in the valley after his own cottage.

The valley gave him friendship too in the quiet forms of Anna and Martin and the farmer George.

The peaks of the highest mountains caught the morning light first before anything down in the valley.
Below the tall peaks the forest covered the middle mountain slopes in a dense and living green layer.

Bird species visited and left the valley forest according to the changing season and the temperature.

From the north came the coldest and most powerful winds Leo had ever felt in any forest or mountain.
North winds bent the forest treetops violently and made low and strange and frightening sounds at night.
At night in the highest winds Leo lay warmly in his bed and listened to the dark forest outside.
Inside the warm cottage the wind was only sound but outside it was a powerful and freezing physical force.
Powerful winter storms had brought down several great old trees in the forest over Leo's many years.
One great storm nearly ten years before had fallen fifteen large trees in a single frightening night.
That terrible night of the great storm had been the most frightening night of Leo's long valley life.
He had sat quietly with Max pressed close beside the warm and flickering fire all through that night.
Trees falling in the storm made a sound like a deep and groaning crack followed by a long silence.
A sound like that stayed inside Leo's memory for many years after the storm and the forest had healed.

Alive in a way that Leo found remarkable and deeply moving on every single morning walk through it.
Through it all the ancient old oak had stood and survived every storm and every season without damage.
The forest edge where the great oak stood was where Leo began and ended each of his daily walks.
His daily walks varied in length from two miles on the shortest days to eight miles on the longest.
The longest summer days allowed the light to last until nearly ten o clock every golden evening.
Evening light in midsummer gave everything in the valley a warm and beautiful amber colored glow.
